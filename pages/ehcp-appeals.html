EHCP Appeals & SEND Tribunal Support

When your local authority gets it wrong, we help you challenge their decision with expert advocacy and proven appeal strategies.

Understanding Your Right to Appeal

Under the Children and Families Act 2014, parents and young people have the right to appeal certain decisions made by local authorities about Education, Health and Care Plans (EHCPs) to the First-tier Tribunal (Special Educational Needs and Disability).

In 2025, over 21,000 appeals were brought to the SEND Tribunal, with families winning in 95% of cases. This extraordinary win rate demonstrates that local authorities frequently make incorrect decisions — and that challenging those decisions works.

Our experienced advocates help you navigate the appeal process with confidence, preparing robust cases that give your child the best chance of receiving the support they need and deserve.

Types of EHCP Appeals We Handle

Refusal to Assess Appeals

If your local authority refuses to carry out an EHC needs assessment (EHCNA), you have the right to appeal within two months of the decision. Local authorities must agree to assess if there is reason to believe a child may have SEN and may need an EHCP — a relatively low threshold that is often incorrectly applied.

What we do:

  • Review the refusal decision and advise on merits
  • Prepare all documents for tribunal registration
  • Advise on evidence gathering and legal arguments
  • Liaise with the Tribunal, school, and local authority
  • Provide ongoing advice until the appeal concludes

EHCP Refusal Appeals

If the local authority conducts an EHC needs assessment but then refuses to issue an EHCP, you can appeal this decision. This often happens when the authority claims the child's needs can be met from within existing school resources — even when the evidence suggests otherwise.

What we do:

  • Analyse the assessment reports and refusal reasoning
  • Prepare all documents required for the appeal
  • Advise on the law and make recommendations on evidence and tactics
  • Provide professional support throughout the appeal process
  • Negotiate with the local authority where appropriate

EHCP Content Appeals

Even once an EHCP is issued, it may not accurately describe your child's needs or specify the right provision. You can appeal Sections B (SEN), F (SEN provision), and I (placement) of the plan to the SEND Tribunal.

What we do:

  • Conduct detailed analysis of the EHCP against legal requirements
  • Draft comprehensive Grounds of Appeal
  • Advise on and draft amendments to the Working Document
  • Review and advise on expert reports
  • Support through to Final Hearing Bundle deadline

The EHCP Appeal Process

Understanding the timeline and key stages of a SEND Tribunal appeal.

Mediation Certificate

Before you can appeal, you must either attend mediation or obtain a certificate confirming you've contacted the mediation service. We guide you through this requirement.

Register Appeal

The appeal must be registered with the Tribunal within two months of the decision (or one month of the mediation certificate). We prepare and submit everything for you.

Case Preparation

We gather evidence, draft submissions, prepare the Working Document, and build your case over the following weeks. This is where thorough preparation makes the difference.

Hearing & Resolution

Many cases settle before reaching a full hearing. If yours goes to tribunal, we can represent you in person. The Tribunal panel makes a legally binding decision.

Key Facts About SEND Tribunal Appeals

95% Win Rate

Parents who appeal to the SEND Tribunal win in approximately 95% of cases, reflecting the frequency of incorrect LA decisions.

20+ Week Timeline

From lodging an appeal to a hearing typically takes around 20 weeks, though many cases settle earlier through negotiation.

No Cost Order Risk

The Tribunal does not normally make cost orders, meaning you won't be asked to pay the local authority's costs if your appeal is unsuccessful.

Legally Binding

Tribunal decisions are legally binding on the local authority. If you win, the LA must implement the Tribunal's order — there are legal consequences if they don't.
