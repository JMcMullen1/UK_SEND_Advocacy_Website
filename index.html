Frequently Asked Questions About SEND & Education Advocacy

An Education, Health and Care Plan (EHCP) is a legal document under the Children and Families Act 2014 for children and young people aged 0 to 25 who need more support than is available through standard SEN support in schools. It sets out your child's special educational needs across the four broad areas of SEN (communication and interaction, cognition and learning, social emotional and mental health, and sensory and physical needs), the specified and quantified provision required in Sections B and F, and the outcomes to be achieved. Children with conditions such as autism, ADHD, dyslexia, or speech and language difficulties may benefit from an EHCP, which provides legally enforceable rights to support — meaning the local authority must ensure that provision is delivered.

SEN support is the first level of additional help a school provides through the graduated approach (Assess, Plan, Do, Review) coordinated by the school's SENCO. It is not legally binding. An EHCP is a legal document that provides enforceable rights to specific, named provision for your child under the Children and Families Act 2014. If SEN support is not meeting your child's needs, you may need to request an EHC needs assessment. Our SEN advocates can advise you on whether an EHCP application is appropriate and help you through the process.

If your child's school is not delivering the provision specified in their EHCP, the local authority remains legally responsible for ensuring it is provided. Schools have a duty of best endeavours under SEND law. You can raise this with the SENCO, the local authority SEN team, or use the formal complaints process. In cases of persistent failure, you may have grounds for a disability discrimination claim under the Equality Act 2010. Our education advocates can advise you on the most effective course of action and support you through any formal processes.
